PREVENTIVE CARE AND SCREENING GUIDELINES
Clinical Practice Summary (Demo Document — Not Medical Advice)

CANCER SCREENING

Colorectal Cancer Screening (USPSTF 2021):
- Start screening at age 45 (previously 50)
- Continue through age 75 for all average-risk adults
- Ages 76–85: individualize decision based on health status and prior screening history

Acceptable screening modalities:
Annual:
- High-sensitivity guaiac fecal occult blood test (HSgFOBT)
- Fecal immunochemical test (FIT)

Every 1–3 years:
- FIT-DNA (stool DNA test, e.g., Cologuard): every 1–3 years

Every 5 years:
- CT colonography (virtual colonoscopy)
- Flexible sigmoidoscopy

Every 10 years:
- Colonoscopy (gold standard; allows polypectomy at same visit)

High-risk individuals (Lynch syndrome, IBD, strong family history):
- Start at younger age; shorter intervals; colonoscopy preferred

Breast Cancer Screening (USPSTF 2024 update):
- Biennial mammography starting at age 40 (updated from 50)
- Continue through age 74
- High-risk patients (BRCA1/2 mutations, dense breasts, family history):
  Annual mammography + breast MRI

Cervical Cancer Screening (USPSTF):
- Age 21–65: Pap smear every 3 years
- Age 30–65: Pap + HPV co-test every 5 years (preferred)
  OR HPV primary testing every 5 years
- Discontinue after 65 if adequate prior negative screening
- After total hysterectomy for benign disease: no further screening needed

Lung Cancer Screening (USPSTF 2021):
- Annual low-dose CT (LDCT) chest scan
- Adults aged 50–80 with ≥20 pack-year smoking history
- Current smokers OR quit within past 15 years
- Discontinue if develop condition limiting curative therapy or haven't smoked ≥15 years

Prostate Cancer Screening:
- PSA testing is NOT universally recommended
- USPSTF: individualized decision for men aged 55–69 after shared decision-making
- Men at higher risk (African American, family history): discuss starting at age 40–45

IMMUNIZATIONS FOR ADULTS

Annual vaccines:
- Influenza vaccine: all adults annually, preferably before October
  (High-dose or adjuvanted formulations preferred for adults ≥65)

Routine adult vaccines (catch-up if not previously vaccinated):
- Tdap: once as adult, then Td booster every 10 years
  (Tdap with each pregnancy, regardless of prior immunization)
- MMR: 2 doses if born after 1957 and no documentation of immunity
- Varicella: 2 doses if no history of disease or vaccination
- HPV vaccine: through age 26 routinely; consider ages 27–45 (shared decision)

Vaccines for adults aged ≥50:
- Zoster (shingles) vaccine — Shingrix: 2 doses 2–6 months apart; preferred over Zostavax
  (Recommended for all immunocompetent adults ≥50; also for immunocompromised ≥19)

Vaccines for adults aged ≥65:
- Pneumococcal: PCV20 (Prevnar 20) — single dose
  OR PCV15 followed by PPSV23 (1 year later if not previously vaccinated)
- Influenza: high-dose or adjuvanted flu vaccine preferred
- COVID-19: updated vaccine annually per CDC

Special situations:
- Pregnancy: influenza, Tdap, COVID-19 recommended
- Asplenia: pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib vaccines
- HIV: avoid live vaccines if CD4 < 200; pneumococcal, influenza important

OSTEOPOROSIS SCREENING AND PREVENTION
- DEXA scan (bone mineral density): women ≥65; men ≥70
- Earlier screening: postmenopausal women or men 50–69 with risk factors
  (low body weight, prior fragility fracture, glucocorticoid use, smoking, heavy alcohol)
- FRAX tool: to estimate 10-year fracture probability

Prevention:
- Calcium: 1000–1200 mg/day through diet ± supplements
- Vitamin D: 800–1000 IU/day; adjust based on serum level
- Weight-bearing exercise
- Fall prevention strategies
- Avoid smoking and excess alcohol

Treatment (when indicated by DEXA + FRAX):
- Bisphosphonates (first-line): alendronate, risedronate, zoledronic acid
- Denosumab: SC injection every 6 months; alternative for renal disease
- Teriparatide: anabolic agent for severe osteoporosis

BACK PAIN — ACUTE LOW BACK PAIN
Initial management (first 4–6 weeks):
- Reassurance: >90% improve within 4 weeks
- Remain active; avoid bed rest
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen): first-line pharmacotherapy
- Skeletal muscle relaxants: for acute muscle spasm (short-term)
- Heat application may provide short-term relief

Imaging (X-ray, MRI, CT):
- NOT recommended in first 4–6 weeks without red flag symptoms
- Red flags warranting immediate imaging: cauda equina syndrome, malignancy,
  infection (fever + back pain), significant trauma, neurological deficits

Physical therapy:
- Referral recommended if no improvement at 4–6 weeks
- Core strengthening, posture, manual therapy

Opioids:
- Avoid in acute low back pain; reserve for severe refractory cases
- If used: lowest dose, short duration, frequent reassessment

Chronic low back pain (> 12 weeks):
- Multidisciplinary pain management
- Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) shown effective
- Duloxetine for chronic pain component
