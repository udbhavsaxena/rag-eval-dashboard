HYPERTENSION MANAGEMENT GUIDELINES
Clinical Practice Summary (Demo Document — Not Medical Advice)

DEFINITION AND CLASSIFICATION
Hypertension is defined as a sustained elevation in systolic blood pressure (SBP) ≥130 mmHg
or diastolic blood pressure (DBP) ≥80 mmHg, based on an average of ≥2 careful readings
on ≥2 occasions (ACC/AHA 2017 guidelines).

Blood Pressure Classification:
- Normal:        SBP < 120 AND DBP < 80 mmHg
- Elevated:      SBP 120–129 AND DBP < 80 mmHg
- Stage 1 HTN:   SBP 130–139 OR DBP 80–89 mmHg
- Stage 2 HTN:   SBP ≥140 OR DBP ≥90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: SBP >180 and/or DBP >120 mmHg

BLOOD PRESSURE TARGETS
For most adults with hypertension: target < 130/80 mmHg.
For adults with high cardiovascular risk (10-year ASCVD risk ≥10%): target < 130/80 mmHg.
For adults aged ≥65 with hypertension: treat to SBP < 130 mmHg if tolerated.
For adults with CKD: target < 130/80 mmHg to slow progression of kidney disease.
For adults with diabetes and hypertension: target < 130/80 mmHg.

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS (Non-Pharmacological Treatment)
All patients with elevated BP or hypertension should be counseled on:

1. DASH Diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension):
   - Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy
   - Reduced saturated fat, total fat, and sodium
   - Expected BP reduction: 8–14 mmHg systolic

2. Sodium Restriction:
   - Reduce sodium intake to < 1500 mg/day (optimal)
   - Aim for < 2300 mg/day at minimum
   - Expected BP reduction: 2–8 mmHg

3. Physical Activity:
   - At least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity aerobic activity
   - Or 75 minutes per week of vigorous-intensity activity
   - Expected BP reduction: 4–9 mmHg

4. Weight Reduction:
   - Aim for BMI < 25 kg/m²
   - Expected BP reduction: 5–20 mmHg per 10 kg weight loss

5. Alcohol Moderation:
   - Men: ≤2 drinks/day; Women: ≤1 drink/day
   - Expected BP reduction: 2–4 mmHg

6. Smoking Cessation:
   - Strongly recommended; smoking increases cardiovascular risk substantially

PHARMACOLOGICAL TREATMENT
First-line agents for most patients:

Thiazide diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone):
- Effective for most patients
- Chlorthalidone preferred over HCTZ for cardiovascular outcomes

ACE Inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril):
- Preferred in patients with CKD, diabetes with proteinuria, or heart failure
- Avoid in pregnancy (teratogenic)

Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) (e.g., losartan, valsartan):
- Alternative to ACE inhibitors (better tolerated; no cough side effect)
- Preferred in patients who cannot tolerate ACE inhibitor cough

Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs) (e.g., amlodipine, diltiazem):
- Effective in older adults and patients with isolated systolic hypertension
- Dihydropyridines preferred (amlodipine)

Beta-blockers (e.g., metoprolol, carvedilol):
- Not first-line for hypertension unless concurrent indication (e.g., angina, HFrEF)
- Useful for patients post-MI or with atrial fibrillation

RESISTANT HYPERTENSION
Defined as BP remaining above goal despite use of ≥3 antihypertensive medications
(including a diuretic) at maximum tolerated doses.
Evaluation should include:
- Rule out secondary causes (primary hyperaldosteronism, renal artery stenosis, OSA)
- Confirm medication adherence
- Check white coat effect (ambulatory BP monitoring)
Additional agents: aldosterone antagonist (spironolactone), alpha-blockers, central agents

MONITORING AND FOLLOW-UP
- Recheck BP in 1 month after initiating or changing therapy
- Once stable, every 3–6 months
- Monitor electrolytes and renal function when using ACE inhibitors, ARBs, or diuretics
- Assess overall cardiovascular risk annually

HYPERTENSIVE URGENCY AND EMERGENCY
Hypertensive Urgency: SBP >180 or DBP >120 without acute end-organ damage
- Oral medications; goal to reduce BP over 24–48 hours
- Oral labetalol or clonidine commonly used

Hypertensive Emergency: severe BP elevation WITH end-organ damage
(stroke, MI, aortic dissection, pulmonary edema, hypertensive encephalopathy)
- IV medications in monitored setting
- Reduce MAP by ≤25% in first hour; avoid rapid falls
- IV agents: labetalol, nicardipine, sodium nitroprusside
