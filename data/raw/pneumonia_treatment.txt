COMMUNITY-ACQUIRED PNEUMONIA (CAP) TREATMENT GUIDELINES
Clinical Practice Summary (Demo Document — Not Medical Advice)

DEFINITION
Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP) is an acute infection of the pulmonary parenchyma
acquired outside of a healthcare setting, in patients who have not been recently hospitalized
or in long-term care facilities.

DIAGNOSIS
Clinical presentation:
- New cough (productive or non-productive)
- Fever, chills, rigors
- Pleuritic chest pain
- Dyspnea, tachypnea
- Physical exam: dullness to percussion, bronchial breath sounds, crackles

Radiographic confirmation:
- Chest X-ray: new infiltrate required for definitive diagnosis
- CT chest: higher sensitivity; useful if CXR equivocal or complications suspected

Laboratory workup (for hospitalized patients):
- CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel
- Blood cultures × 2 (before antibiotics if possible)
- Sputum Gram stain and culture
- Urinary antigen tests (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Legionella pneumophila)
- Procalcitonin (to guide antibiotic duration)

SEVERITY ASSESSMENT — PSI / PORT Score and CURB-65
CURB-65 criteria (1 point each):
- Confusion (new onset)
- Urea > 7 mmol/L (BUN > 19 mg/dL)
- Respiratory rate ≥ 30/min
- Blood pressure (SBP < 90 or DBP ≤ 60 mmHg)
- Age ≥ 65 years

Score interpretation:
- 0–1: Low risk; outpatient treatment
- 2: Intermediate risk; consider short-stay inpatient
- 3–5: High risk; hospitalize; consider ICU if ≥4

ANTIBIOTIC TREATMENT

Outpatient Treatment (mild CAP, healthy patient, no recent antibiotics):
First-line options:
1. Amoxicillin 1g PO three times daily × 5–7 days (preferred for low-risk typical CAP)
2. Doxycycline 100 mg PO twice daily × 5 days (if atypical pathogen suspected)
3. Azithromycin 500 mg day 1, then 250 mg days 2–5 (macrolide; consider local resistance)
   - Note: Macrolide monotherapy only if pneumococcal resistance < 25% locally

Outpatient Treatment (comorbidities: COPD, diabetes, renal/liver disease, alcoholism):
- Combination therapy:
  - Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg PO twice daily + azithromycin or doxycycline
  - OR respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin 750 mg PO daily or moxifloxacin 400 mg PO daily)
  - Duration: 5 days (if clinical stability achieved)

Inpatient (Non-ICU) Treatment:
1. Beta-lactam + macrolide:
   - Ampicillin-sulbactam 3g IV every 6h + azithromycin 500 mg IV/PO daily
   - OR ceftriaxone 1–2 g IV daily + azithromycin 500 mg daily
2. Respiratory fluoroquinolone monotherapy:
   - Levofloxacin 750 mg IV/PO daily
   - Moxifloxacin 400 mg IV/PO daily

Inpatient (ICU) Treatment:
- Beta-lactam + macrolide or respiratory fluoroquinolone
- If Pseudomonas risk: anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime)
  + ciprofloxacin or aminoglycoside
- If MRSA risk or prior MRSA: add vancomycin or linezolid

SPECIAL PATHOGENS
Legionella pneumophila:
- Atypical presentation: GI symptoms, hyponatremia, high fever, elevated LFTs
- Treatment: levofloxacin, azithromycin, or doxycycline; NOT beta-lactams

Pneumocystis jirovecii (PCP) — in immunocompromised:
- Treatment: TMP-SMX (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole)
- Add corticosteroids if PaO2 < 70 mmHg

Aspiration Pneumonia:
- Typically anaerobes ± gram-negatives
- Amoxicillin-clavulanate or piperacillin-tazobactam; metronidazole can be added

DURATION OF THERAPY
- Minimum 5 days; patient must be afebrile × 48–72 hours and clinically stable
- Extend to 7–10 days for bacteremic CAP, MRSA, or Legionella
- Use procalcitonin to guide de-escalation

RESPONSE TO TREATMENT
- Most patients improve within 3–5 days
- Failure to improve: consider resistant organisms, unusual pathogens, complications
  (empyema, parapneumonic effusion, lung abscess), or alternative diagnoses
- Follow-up chest imaging at 7–12 weeks post-discharge (especially in smokers >50)

PREVENTION
- Pneumococcal vaccines (PCV15/PCV20 and PPSV23): adults ≥65, immunocompromised
- Influenza vaccine: annually for all adults
- Smoking cessation strongly recommended
